# Direct vs Regular Plans: The Single Biggest Cost Decision

Every Indian mutual fund scheme is sold in two plan variants. **Direct** and
**Regular** plans hold an identical underlying portfolio. They differ only in
distribution cost.

## What sits inside each

| Plan | Distribution channel | Annual fee gap |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct** | You buy from the AMC website or platforms like MF Central, Coin, Kuvera, Groww (mutual funds) | None of your money goes to a distributor |
| **Regular** | You buy through a bank, financial advisor, or distributor | An annual trail commission (typically 0.50 to 1.00 percent) is paid to the distributor from the scheme's assets |

The fee gap is built into the daily NAV. You do not see a separate line item.

## Why this matters more than fund selection

A 0.75 percent annual fee gap, compounded over 20 years, costs about **15 to 18
percent** of the final corpus. That is more than the alpha most active managers
deliver. For a long-horizon SIP, switching from Regular to Direct is the single
highest-leverage decision you can make.

### Worked example

* SIP of INR 10,000 per month for 20 years
* Underlying fund delivers a gross 12 percent CAGR before any fees
* Direct plan expense ratio 0.80 percent. Net 11.20 percent. Final value approx INR 99 lakh
* Regular plan expense ratio 1.55 percent. Net 10.45 percent. Final value approx INR 91 lakh
* Difference: about INR 8 lakh, paid to the distributor

## How to identify and switch

* Every scheme name should contain "Direct" or "Regular" plus the option (Growth
  or IDCW). If it does not say Direct, it is Regular
* Switching from Regular to Direct within the same scheme is a redemption-cum-
  fresh-purchase event for tax purposes. Equity funds held longer than 12 months
  trigger LTCG; debt funds trigger slab-rate tax
* The cleanest path is to **stop new contributions** to Regular and start new
  SIPs in Direct. Hold the existing Regular units until LTCG harvesting opportunity
  or sell after the lock-in (for ELSS)

## When Regular plans can still make sense

* You genuinely want and use a SEBI-registered investment adviser (RIA) who
  charges a transparent fee. In that case, prefer Direct plans plus an RIA
  flat fee, not Regular plans + commission
* You are a complete beginner and the bank "relationship manager" walked you
  through your first SIP. The right move at year 2 is to learn enough to switch
  to Direct
