**Short answer: don't stop.** A market drawdown is precisely the period when
SIPs accumulate the most units per rupee. Stopping converts a temporary paper
loss into a permanent missed recovery.

**The arithmetic of staying invested:**

* INR 5,000 monthly SIP for 10 years at 12 percent gross compounds to about
  INR 11.6 lakh
* Pausing for 12 months mid-cycle and then resuming takes the corpus down by
  roughly INR 1.4 lakh at the end of 10 years (and more if those 12 months
  happened to be the deepest drawdown of the decade, when units would have
  been cheapest)

**What actually happened in past crashes:**

* SIPs that ran continuously through the 2008 to 2009 GFC drawdown beat SIPs
  that paused for 6 to 12 months by a meaningful margin over the next 5 years
* Same pattern in March 2020. Investors who paused after the initial crash
  missed the fastest recovery in modern history

**When you might genuinely consider pausing (rare):**

* You lost your job and need to preserve cash for the emergency fund. In that
  case, pause new contributions; do **not** redeem existing units
* You discovered the fund itself has a serious red flag (manager exit, AUM
  bloat in a small-cap context, regulatory action). In that case, switch to
  another fund, do not exit the asset class

**The behavioural rule of thumb:** If you cannot stomach a 30 to 40 percent
paper drawdown without panic, your equity allocation is too high for your
risk tolerance, not the other way around. Rebalance permanently after this
drawdown ends, not during it.
