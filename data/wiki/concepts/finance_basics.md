# Personal Finance & Investing — First Principles Primer

> This page is the onboarding primer the advisor agent reads whenever a new
> user asks "how do I get started / where should I invest". It is deliberately
> opinion-free at the individual-stock level — opinions come later, from the
> live wiki pages, once the user understands the vocabulary below.

## 1. What a "stock" actually is

When you buy a stock you are buying a **fractional share of ownership** in a
company. If Apple has ~15 billion shares outstanding and you own 15, you own a
one-billionth slice of Apple's future profits and assets. Two ways that slice
pays you:

- **Capital appreciation** — the share price goes up because the business
  becomes more valuable (or more popular).
- **Dividends** — some companies hand back part of their profits each quarter
  as cash. Mature businesses (Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson, utilities) tend to
  pay dividends. Fast-growing ones (Tesla, Amazon pre-2024) usually don't.

## 2. Stocks vs bonds vs cash vs "alternatives"

| Asset class | What it is | Typical long-term return | Typical risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cash / money-market** | USD, T-bills, HYSA | ~matches inflation | Lowest |
| **Bonds** | Loans to governments or companies | 3–5 % real | Medium |
| **Stocks** | Ownership of businesses | 6–7 % real | Higher |
| **Real estate / REITs** | Buildings and rent | 4–6 % real | Medium–high |
| **Commodities / crypto / VC** | Gold, oil, BTC, private startups | Highly variable | Very high |

Two rules of thumb:
- Higher expected return almost always comes with higher **volatility** — the
  chance your account value swings around in the short term.
- In the long run (20+ years), a diversified basket of stocks has beaten every
  other mainstream asset class — but it has lost 30–50 % in individual years
  more than once. You need the emotional stomach for that.

## 3. Risk and return, explained properly

"Risk" is not a feeling. It is a **distribution of outcomes** around your
expected return. An S&P 500 index fund has an expected annual return of
roughly 7 % after inflation, but the distribution around that number is
wide — any given year, returns can realistically fall anywhere between –40 %
and +40 %. Longer horizons collapse the distribution: the 20-year real
return of the U.S. stock market has almost never been negative.

Practical implications:

- Money you need in **less than 3 years** should not be in stocks. Use a
  high-yield savings account or short-term treasuries.
- Money you will not touch for **10+ years** can tolerate stock-level risk.
- The same investment can be "safe" or "risky" depending on your time horizon.

## 4. Diversification — the only free lunch

Two stocks in different industries do not move together perfectly. Combine
many of them and the portfolio's volatility drops *without* dropping the
expected return as much. This is why **index funds exist**: instead of picking
3 stocks and hoping, you buy a tiny slice of all ~500 large U.S. companies
(S&P 500) or all ~4000 global public companies (total world index).

Rule of thumb: if you can explain the argument for every individual stock you
own in one sentence, fine. If you can't, you should be in an index fund.

## 5. Compound interest is the whole game

If you invest \$500/month starting at age 25 and earn 7 % real per year, you
have ~\$1.2 M at 65. Start at age 35 with the same \$500/month and you have
~\$570 k. The **10-year delay costs you more than half your final number**.
The earliest dollar you invest is worth many times the last one, because it
compounds longer.

Corollary: *time in the market beats timing the market*. Missing the best 10
trading days in any decade cuts long-term returns roughly in half.

## 6. Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)

Instead of putting a lump sum in on day one, you invest the same dollar
amount on the same day every week or month. Two effects:

- You buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they are high — your
  average cost-per-share is lower than the naïve average price.
- You remove the emotional "is now the right time" decision entirely.

For a beginner, automated DCA into a broad index fund is — statistically —
a very hard strategy to beat.

## 7. Account types (U.S.-centric)

- **401(k) / 403(b)** — employer-sponsored retirement account. If your
  employer matches contributions, **contribute at least enough to get the
  full match**. That match is a 100 % instant return — nothing else in
  finance offers that.
- **Roth IRA** — you pay tax on the contribution today, but every dollar of
  growth is tax-free forever. Ideal for young investors in low tax brackets.
- **Traditional IRA** — tax-deductible today, taxed on withdrawal. Better
  for high earners who expect to be in a lower bracket at retirement.
- **Taxable brokerage** — no tax perks, but no contribution limits and no
  age restrictions on withdrawals. Use after the tax-advantaged accounts are
  full.
- **HSA** — if you have a high-deductible health plan, this is the only
  U.S. account that is tax-free on contribution *and* growth *and* qualified
  withdrawals. Triple tax advantage.

## 8. A reasonable starter playbook

The advisor agent can personalise this from live data, but the template is:

1. **Emergency fund first** — 3–6 months of essential expenses in a high-yield
   savings account. Before any investing.
2. **Pay off high-interest debt** — credit cards at 20 %+ APR beat every
   investment return. Knock these out.
3. **Capture the 401(k) match** — lowest-hanging fruit in all of personal
   finance.
4. **Max a Roth IRA** — if eligible; default it to a total-world or target-
   date index fund.
5. **Top up the 401(k) or open a taxable brokerage** — again default to
   broad, low-cost index funds (total-market ETFs like VTI/ITOT, or
   target-date funds for auto-rebalancing).
6. **Only after all of the above**: consider individual stocks, only with
   money you can afford to lose, and only once you can articulate *why* the
   business you are buying will be worth more later.

## 9. Vocabulary the advisor will use

- **P/E ratio** — price divided by earnings-per-share. Rough measure of how
  expensive a stock is relative to its profits. S&P 500 long-run average
  ~15–20. Higher means the market expects growth; lower can mean cheap or
  broken.
- **Market cap** — share price × total shares. Large cap = > \$10 B, mid
  cap = \$2–10 B, small cap = < \$2 B.
- **Volatility** — how much the price swings. Often quoted as standard
  deviation of daily returns.
- **Drawdown** — % drop from the most recent peak. S&P has had 50 %+
  drawdowns twice in the last 25 years.
- **Expense ratio** — annual fee an ETF or mutual fund charges, as a % of
  assets. Under 0.1 % is cheap; above 0.5 % is expensive.
- **Sharpe ratio** — return per unit of volatility. Higher = better
  risk-adjusted performance.
- **Dividend yield** — annual dividend / share price, as a %.

## 10. Common beginner mistakes (avoid these)

1. **Picking stocks you hear about on social media** without reading a 10-K.
2. **Panic-selling in a crash** — the average investor underperforms the
   market by 2–3 %/yr purely because they buy high and sell low.
3. **Chasing last year's winners** — sector leadership rotates; yesterday's
   best performer is often tomorrow's worst.
4. **Ignoring fees** — a 1 % expense ratio over 40 years eats ~25 % of your
   final portfolio.
5. **No written plan** — if you don't have a target asset allocation, you
   drift into whatever feels comfortable today.
