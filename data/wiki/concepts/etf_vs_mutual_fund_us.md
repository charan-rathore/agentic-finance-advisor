# ETF vs Mutual Fund (US Edition)

In the US, both Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) and Mutual Funds are pooled
investment vehicles regulated under the Investment Company Act of 1940. They
hold portfolios of stocks, bonds, or other assets but differ in how investors
buy and sell them and how they are taxed.

## Key structural differences

| Attribute | ETF | Mutual Fund |
|---|---|---|
| Trading | Trades intraday on a stock exchange like a stock | Buys and sells once per day at NAV calculated after market close |
| Pricing | Real-time market price (small premium / discount to NAV possible) | NAV-based, no intraday pricing |
| Minimum investment | Price of one share (some brokers offer fractional shares) | Often USD 500 to USD 3,000 minimum |
| Expense ratios | Generally lower; broad-market index ETFs at 0.03 to 0.10 percent | Index mutual funds 0.04 to 0.20 percent; active mutual funds 0.50 to 1.00 percent |
| Tax efficiency | Higher in taxable accounts due to in-kind creation/redemption process | Lower; capital gains distributions can occur even if you did not sell |
| Account types | Brokerage accounts, IRAs, 401(k)s (where offered) | Brokerage, IRAs, 401(k) menus |

## Why ETFs are generally more tax-efficient

ETFs use an "in-kind" creation and redemption mechanism with authorized
participants. When investors leave the fund, the AP exchanges baskets of
underlying securities rather than triggering a sale. This avoids realising
capital gains at the fund level. Mutual funds, in contrast, sell securities
to meet redemptions and may distribute the resulting gains to all shareholders
proportionally.

In tax-advantaged accounts (401(k), IRA), this difference largely disappears.

## Where each makes sense

* **ETFs** are typically the better choice for taxable brokerage accounts due
  to the tax efficiency advantage
* **Mutual funds** remain dominant in 401(k) menus because plan sponsors and
  recordkeepers are built around the mutual fund infrastructure
* For cash sitting in IRAs, either works. The deciding factor is fund cost and
  fit for purpose

## Things to watch

* Bid-ask spread on illiquid ETFs can be a hidden cost
* Some actively managed mutual funds have multiple share classes (A, B, C,
  Investor, Admiral) with different fees and load structures. Read the
  prospectus
* "ETF wrappers" of active strategies (semi-transparent active ETFs) are
  newer and may have different mechanics
