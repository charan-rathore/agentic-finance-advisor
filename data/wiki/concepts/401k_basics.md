# 401(k) Basics: Tax-Advantaged Workplace Retirement in the US

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored, tax-advantaged retirement plan defined under
section 401(k) of the US Internal Revenue Code. It is the most common workplace
retirement vehicle in the United States and the primary retirement-saving
mechanism for most American workers.

## Two flavours

| Type | When you pay tax |
|---|---|
| **Traditional 401(k)** | Contributions are pre-tax, reducing taxable income now. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income |
| **Roth 401(k)** | Contributions are post-tax (no deduction now). Qualified withdrawals in retirement, including all gains, are tax-free |

Many employers offer both. You can split contributions between Traditional and
Roth subject to the combined annual limit.

## Contribution limits for tax year 2025 (per IRS)

* Employee elective deferral limit: **USD 23,500**
* Catch-up contribution for ages 50 plus: additional **USD 7,500**
* Catch-up contribution for ages 60-63 (SECURE 2.0 enhancement): up to **USD 11,250**
  in plans that adopt the higher tier
* Combined employee plus employer limit: **USD 70,000** (or USD 77,500 with
  age-50 catch-up)

These limits are indexed for inflation. Always verify against the current IRS
publication before maxing out a plan.

## Employer match

Most employers offer a match, typically structured as "100 percent of the first
3 to 6 percent of salary you contribute." The match is effectively additional
compensation. Failing to capture the full match leaves money on the table.

## Vesting

The employer-match portion may be subject to a vesting schedule (cliff or
graded) of up to 6 years. Employee contributions are always 100 percent vested
immediately.

## Investment options

The employer chooses the investment menu. Typical options include:

* Target-date funds (index- or actively-managed lifecycle funds)
* Domestic and international equity index funds
* Bond index funds
* Stable value or money market funds
* Company stock (concentration risk if used heavily)

## Withdrawals

* Penalty-free withdrawals begin at age **59 1/2**
* Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) start at age **73** (for individuals
  turning 73 in 2023 or later, per SECURE 2.0)
* Early withdrawals before 59 1/2 typically incur a 10 percent penalty plus
  ordinary income tax, with limited exceptions (hardship, first home, qualified
  medical expenses, separation from service at age 55 plus)
