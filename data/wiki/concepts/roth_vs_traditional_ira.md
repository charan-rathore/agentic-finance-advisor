# Roth IRA vs Traditional IRA: Which to Choose

An Individual Retirement Arrangement (IRA) is a tax-advantaged personal
retirement account in the United States. It is opened directly by an individual,
separate from any employer plan. Two flavours dominate.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Attribute | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution tax treatment | Often deductible now (subject to income limits if covered by a workplace plan) | After-tax (no deduction) |
| Growth | Tax-deferred | Tax-free |
| Qualified withdrawal | Taxed as ordinary income | Tax-free |
| Required Minimum Distributions | Yes, starting at age 73 | No, during the original owner's lifetime |
| Early withdrawal penalty | 10 percent before age 59 1/2 (with exceptions) | Contributions can be withdrawn anytime tax- and penalty-free; earnings have age and 5-year rules |

## Contribution limits for tax year 2025 (per IRS)

* Annual contribution limit: **USD 7,000**
* Age 50 plus catch-up: additional **USD 1,000** (total USD 8,000)
* Limit applies across all your IRAs combined, both Traditional and Roth

## Roth IRA income phase-outs (2025, per IRS)

* Single filers: phased out between modified AGI of approximately USD 150,000
  to USD 165,000
* Married filing jointly: phased out between approximately USD 236,000 to
  USD 246,000

If your income exceeds the upper bound, you cannot contribute directly to a
Roth IRA. The "Backdoor Roth" approach (contribute to a non-deductible
Traditional IRA, then convert) is the common workaround, subject to the
pro-rata rule.

## Decision heuristic

* **Choose Roth** if you expect your tax rate in retirement to be **higher**
  than today (early career, lower current bracket, expecting income growth)
* **Choose Traditional** if you expect your tax rate in retirement to be
  **lower** than today (late-career high earner expecting a step-down in
  retirement)
* When uncertain, splitting contributions across both is a defensible diversifier
  on tax-rate risk

## Where this fits in the broader stack

A common US "retirement waterfall":

1. Contribute to the 401(k) up to the employer match
2. Max out an HSA if eligible (triple tax advantage)
3. Max out a Roth IRA (or Traditional IRA, depending on bracket)
4. Continue 401(k) contributions to the annual limit
5. Taxable brokerage account for additional savings
